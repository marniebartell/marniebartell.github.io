Family doctor check-in

Mobile application and responsive website design

Summary

I was the lead UX designer for a "design for social good" project and created a mobile app and responsive website that simplifies the family doctor check-in process so that patients can:

  • book appointments
  • check-in to their family doctor's office
  • answer initial health screening questions at their convenience

Background

My role

Lead UX designer responsible for all phases of the design process from empathizing, defining, ideating, prototyping, through to testing

Goal

Save time and reduce stress for patients filling out personal information and medical histories

Target users

Young families through to older adults

Pain points

Patients are often frustrated by:

  • Being pressed for time
  • Wanting to minimize exposure to other patients
  • Feeling pressure to remember medical histories on the spot
  • Repeatedly having to fill out the same forms over and over again

User research

User personas and journey maps

I came up with three user personas and mapped out their journeys through the health system:

  • Reena - a busy single mother
  • Carlos - a retired man who is worried about losing his driver's license
  • Nina - a very busy mother

These three personas represent patients who visit their family doctors including busy families and older patients.

Initial concepts

Rapid sketching

Using the “Crazy Eights” exercise, I aimed at coming up with eight ideas in eight minutes. The goal is to develop many ideas as quickly as possible to push beyond initial ideas.

UX design storyboard

To understand how users might fill out these online forms, I created a storyboard about a young mom filling out the family doctor check-in app quickly and easily.

By comparison, having to fill out paperwork at the doctor's office, with two babies and a busy toddler, would be very frustrating and stressful.

Nina has a young daughter and twin boys that keep her very busy.

Nina opens up her family doctor’s app to sign in from home, in advance of their doctor's appointment.

She checks off her daughter’s allergies.

She can look up her twins’ next vaccinations and indicate which ones she wants them to get next.

After answering questions in advance, she is all checked in.

Nina is happy at the doctor’s office because she can get in right away without having to fill out paperwork.

User testing - Round 1

Method

  • Moderated usability studies
  • Round 1 = low-fidelity prototypes
  • 5 participants
  • 30 minutes each

Round 1 findings

  • Include mental illness
  • Clarify preferred pharmacy
  • Move diabetes to its own section
  • Define medical terms
  • Introduce the personal profile

One thing that testing revealed was that the user profile/history section was missed on the login screen, so I moved it to the top right corner so it could be viewed from every page.

User testing - Round 2

Method

  • Moderated usability studies
  • Round 2 = high-fidelity prototypes
  • 5 participants
  • 30 minutes each

Round 2 findings

  • Make personal profile more visible
  • Add “Forget password”
  • Ensure available days are more apparent
  • Combine screens to better align with 3 steps

One thing the second round of testing revealed was that "forgot password" was missing. This was easy to add and a great improvement.

Results and outcomes

Impact

By designing apps/websites like this one, we can improve patient access to the healthcare system during a crucial time in peoples’ lives when they aren’t feeling well and are especially vulnerable.

Learnings

  • I learned that my assumptions aren’t always correct, but that's okay.
  • Through user testing I was able to learn how users actually use my proposed app and website.
  • Through careful consideration, we can create more usable and accessible products, like this family doctor check-in tool, for an improved user experience.
